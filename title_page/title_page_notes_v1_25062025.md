The characters you’ve quoted are a standard Tibetan paired bracket set in Unicode:

- U+0F3A: ༺ — TIBETAN MARK GUG RTAGS GYON, a left (opening) bracket-like punctuation mark used together with U+0F3B as a pair. [1][2][6]
- U+0F3B: ༻ — TIBETAN MARK GUG RTAGS GYAS, a right (closing) bracket-like punctuation mark, i.e. the closing member of that pair. [1][2][4][6]

In Unicode’s general category these are classified as paired punctuation: U+0F3A is “Ps, open” and U+0F3B is “Pe, close”, functionally analogous to parentheses or brackets in other scripts. [2][6]

- “༺” is the sign called *gug rtags gyon* (literally “hook-mark left”), encoded at u+0f3a. it’s the left / opening member of a tibetan ornamental bracket pair, used much like an opening quote or decorative bracket around titles, phrases, or sometimes mantric material.  
- “༻” is *gug rtags gyas* (“hook-mark right”), at u+0f3b, the matching right / closing sign.  
- they’re treated as paired punctuation in unicode: the first is an opening mark, the second a closing mark, similar in function to “(” and “)”, but visually adapted to tibetan calligraphic style.  
- in traditional layout, they tend to hug the text closely and are not “structural” like the shad (།) used for phrase segmentation; they’re more like decorative framing or emphasis markers around a word, title, or passage.

### how these marks sit with mantras

- using ༺ and ༻ as a pair around mantric text is consistent with their role as decorative, framing punctuation in tibetan writing, especially for auspicious phrases or short titles. [1][2][3]
- they don’t change the “mantric” status the way terma-specific marks like the ter‑tseg ༔ can indicate treasure-origin; they just visually bracket or highlight what’s inside, much like ornate quotes or a cartouche. [4][5][6]

### practical tips for your use

- keep them tight to the mantra with no extra spacing: e.g. ༺ཨོཾ་མ་ཎི་པདྨེ་ཧཱུྃ༻ rather than with spaces after ༺ or before ༻. [2][3]
- if you are mixing them with other special marks (like ༔ at the end of a terma mantra), use the gug rtags as the outermost frame and reserve the more semantically loaded marks for internal, tradition-specific roles. [4][5]
